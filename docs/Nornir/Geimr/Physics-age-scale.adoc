= Age Scale Reference

The `Age` parameter uses a 1–100 scale that maps piecewise-exponentially from 10⁶ years to 10¹³ years, across three zones.
The range approximates the https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Five_Ages_of_the_Universe#Stelliferous_Era[Stelliferous Era] — the span during which stars actively form, from the first stars (~10⁶–10⁸ yr) through the last red dwarfs burning out (~10¹³–10¹⁴ yr) — so the scale covers the full range of ages a simulated star or planet could plausibly have.

[cols="<,<,<"]
|===
|Score range |Exponent range |Age range

|1 – 40 |10⁶ – 10¹⁰ |1 million yr – 10 billion yr
|40 – 70 |10¹⁰ – 10¹¹ |10 billion yr – 100 billion yr
|70 – 100 |10¹¹ – 10¹³ |100 billion yr – 10 trillion yr
|===

Score is linearly interpolated against exponent within each zone, then age = 10^exponent.
Implemented by `Range100.PiecewiseExponentialScale(age, exponents: [6, 10, 11, 13])` (default thresholds `[40, 70, 100]`).
The following table provides a reference for known ages and their corresponding scale values.

[cols=">,>,<"]
|===
|Score |Age (yr) |Reference

|*1* |*1,000,000* |*Scale minimum*
|11 |10,000,000 |Decade
|21 |100,000,000 |Decade
|30 |1,000,000,000 |Decade
|37 |4,600,000,000 |https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth[Solar System age]
|*40* |*10,000,000,000* |*10 billion yr*
|44 |13,800,000,000 |https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe[Age of the universe]
|*70* |*100,000,000,000* |*100 billion yr*
|85 |1,000,000,000,000 |Decade
|*100* |*10,000,000,000,000* |*Scale maximum*
|===

The scale maximum (10 trillion yr) is well beyond the current age of the universe; the upper zone exists to accommodate the projected lifespan of long-lived red dwarfs.
